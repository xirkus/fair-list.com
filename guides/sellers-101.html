Introduction – The Rollercoaster

Selling your property can at best be described as a rollercoaster – full of ups and downs. Success means coming to grips with the fear and elation associated with the process, but the end result can be quite rewarding. While most people wouldn’t characterize selling their homes as “fun”, our goal is to provide you with tools to make the process manageable and preserve as much of your equity as possible. We can walk you through the process from beginning to end, but you should understand that there are many aspects of the process that are under your direct control. Using these to your advantage can significantly improve your chances of success.

Regardless of whether or not you’re in a hot market, getting the most for your home requires understanding what buyers are looking for and getting their interest. While buyers in hot markets are more willing to tolerate imperfections, the principles below apply regardless of the market conditions (which is good for the rest of us).

Advertising vs. Marketing

To successfully sell your property requires understanding the difference between advertising and marketing. Most people (agents included) confuse advertising for marketing and vice versa.

The goal of advertising is to get the buyers attention.

The following are forms of advertising:

  • Listing your property on MLS or aggregator sites like Zillow

  • Putting up a yard sign

  • Holding an open house

  • Listing your property on an agent’s/broker’s web site

These methods provide awareness to potential homebuyers that your property is for sale. They do not communicate what is appealing about your property.

The goal of marketing is to understand what makes your home desirable to a buyer.

Understanding the difference between marketing and advertising is the key to successfully selling your property. Excellent realtors understand that the value that they provide is through their expertise in marketing.

Thankfully, this is something you can learn for yourself.

Marketing – What Makes Your Home Standout?

In markets where supply is severely limited, selling a lot with a burnt out house might still result in a massive payout. For everyone else, marketing requires identifying all the things which appeal to potential buyers. Here are a few of the things that buyers look for when considering location:

  • According to realtor.com 78% of homebuyers with school aged children would choose a home in a good school district over a home with better amenities (such as a large yard or updated kitchen).

  • NAR's 2017 statics show that 57% of homebuyers would forego a home with a larger yard in favor of a shorter commute.

  • If you’re targeting millennials, the character of the neighborhood is a paramount consideration. In a recent survey conducted by Ally Homes, 91% of the surveyed demographic would consider moving to a different community with a better vibe. This means emphasizing accessibility, mobility, and nearby restaurants/coffee shops.

  • Have a waterfront property? These generally sell for a premium regardless of location according to Zillow. However, demand is diminishing for these types of properties as measured by the decreasing premium buyers are willing to pay. This trend may be a result of climate change related concerns and the perceived risk to properties which may be damaged due to extreme weather conditions.

Zillow's recent analysis of home sales found a number of features which can potentially increase the selling price. These include:

  • Homes with front doors painted black or charcoal grey can command premiums of $6000+.

  • Tuxedo kitchens add about $1500 to the selling price.

  • Bathrooms and living rooms in neutral blueish tones added $2700 to the selling price.

  • Does your home have central air conditioning? If so, this could potentially be an additional $5000.

Identifying and showcasing the aspects of your property which make it stand out is at the core of our listing process.

Pricing – An Educated Guess

Over-estimating the price is a sure way to communicate unrealistic expectations to potential buyers. Listing prices, as a rule, can only be adjusted downwards. On the other hand, starting with a lower price may useful to generate initial interest, but unless there is a bidding war, you may also be undervaluing your property.

This begs the question – how do you determine the right price to begin with?

The key to pricing is research and awareness.

Properties recently sold in your area provide a solid basis of comparison to determine your initial price. Unfortunately, historical MLS data is generally not available directly to consumers. Sites such as Zillow can provide this information.

What you’ll come to quickly realize is that pricing is a guessing game.

While systems such as Zestimates™ can provide a ballpark value, there are numerous cases of it being too low or too high. Zillow itself recommends a combination of comparative market assessments, professional appraisals, and in person visits to the property to supplement is calculated estimate. In addition, Zestimates are adjusted to reflect actual listing prices which makes you question the accuracy of their predictions to begin with.

An educated guess on the initial price is the best you can achieve. This is no different than what agents/brokers do. It’s important to understand that the listing price is the starting point for negotiations, not an end in and of itself.

Be prepared to adjust as needed.

Showing – Put You Best Foot Forward

Selling is all about first impressions. There are a number of things that you can do to improve these impressions, but a lot of it comes down preparing your property before the first visit.

  1. Repair any obvious damage which might affect the price.

  2. Apply fresh coat of paint. This goes double for rooms painted red, brown, or yellow as rooms in these colours can also reduce the price by ~$2000 according to Zillow.

  3. Consider hiring a professional stager/photographer for your listing. They can help you declutter and make your home presentation worthy without the emotional baggage.

  4. Clean everything (carpets, windows, yard, garage, HVAC, etc.).

  5. Deodorize the “lived in” smell. Use lemons, fresh flowers, and baking soda to neutralize bad smells. Avoid using artificial deodorants as these have a tendency to only mask the smell.

  6. Make the entrance memorable and inviting.

You can do all these things yourself without having to use an agent. Our in-app checklists can be used to ensure that you haven’t overlooked any important details.

Negotiating – Keeping a Level Head

The best negotiators are empathetic, meaning that they can see another person’s perspective. That does not mean that they are emotional. While negotiation can be seen as a “winner-take-all” challenge, it is more productive to approach it as reaching a beneficial consensus where both parties win by getting what’s important to them. This is especially true for a successful real estate transaction.

Successful negotiations require:

  1. Listening. Without listening, you can’t know what’s important to the homebuyer.

  2. Understanding what you consider non-negotiable and communicating that clearly. This also means being prepared to walk away.

  3. Addressing the other homebuyer’s concerns. This may require both sides to reciprocate with something negotiable.

  4. Being patient. Rushing to a conclusion is not the goal; a mutually beneficial outcome is.

  5. Remembering to be empathetic, but not emotional. Don’t take it personally.

Our Value to You - A New Perspective

The real estate industry as a whole focuses on buyers and agents as the primary customer. They fail to realize that in an era of chronic housing shortages, it's the seller who should receive the focus. After all, you pay the commissions and bear the investment risk. As a seller, you need to ask yourself the question - what is the value of using an agent? Given how much of the selling process is directly dependent on you, do you really need one?

According to NAR's 2017 statistics, 8% of all listings were sold for sale by owner. In the past, this number was as high as 20% of the total volume. What changed? In the same period, agent lead listings have increased from 69% to 87%. Limiting direct access to MLS data by consumers (which NAR officially supports) only helps agents maintain control for their own benefit. Despite the commoditization of search by technology, the real estate industry as a whole has not updated their costs to reflect the diminishing value of their services. Is a buying agent's commission worth it when 51% of all buyers found the home they purchased on the internet?

Of course, not everyone will be comfortable navigating the selling process by themselves. That's OK. The expertise and emotional support is the price of commission charged by agents. For others, they understand that their home is one of their most valuable assets and that giving away a significant amount of their wealth should be considered cautiously.
